Artificial Turf for Daycares and Schools in Georgia

By Dusty Broadhead | March 20, 2026

Children’s outdoor play areas take more abuse than almost any other turf application. Kids run, fall, slide, dig, and generally test every surface to its limits—all day, every day, for months on end. Natural grass simply cannot survive this level of use, which is why bare dirt and mud patches are so common at daycares and schools throughout Georgia. Artificial turf gives these facilities a durable, safe, and always-green play surface that stands up to the toughest users on the planet.

The Problem with Natural Grass at Schools

A typical Georgia daycare with 30-50 children using an outdoor play area daily will destroy a natural grass surface within weeks during the growing season—and even faster during the dormant winter months when grass isn’t growing to recover. The result is compacted dirt that turns to mud when it rains and dust when it’s dry. Neither condition is safe, clean, or enjoyable for children.

Schools face the same challenge on a larger scale. Elementary school playgrounds, PE areas, and recess fields see hundreds of children per day. The high-traffic zones around playground equipment, under swings, and at the bases of slides become bare dirt almost immediately. Many Georgia schools simply accept this as inevitable—but it doesn’t have to be.

Safety Standards and Fall Protection

For daycares and schools, safety isn’t just a preference—it’s a regulatory requirement. The Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) and ASTM International have specific standards for playground surfacing, including critical fall height (CFH) ratings that measure how well a surface absorbs impact from falls.

Artificial turf systems designed for playgrounds include shock-absorbing padding beneath the turf that meets or exceeds these safety standards. The padding thickness is matched to the fall height of the playground equipment—taller equipment requires thicker padding. A properly installed playground turf system can achieve CFH ratings of 8-12 feet, meaning it provides adequate fall protection for most standard playground equipment.

This is a significant improvement over hard-packed dirt (which provides almost no fall protection) and even natural grass over compacted soil. Only engineered wood fiber mulch and rubber surfacing offer comparable fall protection—and both have maintenance and accessibility disadvantages compared to turf.

ADA Accessibility

The Americans with Disabilities Act requires that playground surfaces be accessible to children with mobility devices. Loose-fill surfaces like mulch and pea gravel fail this requirement—wheelchairs and walkers can’t roll through them effectively. Artificial turf with appropriate padding provides a firm, stable surface that meets ADA accessibility requirements while also providing fall protection. This dual capability makes turf the preferred surface for inclusive playgrounds that serve all children.

Cleanliness and Hygiene

For daycares serving young children, cleanliness is a primary concern. Natural grass and dirt harbor insects, allow standing water (mosquito breeding), and create mud that children track inside. Children inevitably fall and put their hands on the ground—and then put those hands near their faces.

Artificial turf eliminates mud, reduces insect habitat, drains quickly to prevent standing water, and can be sanitized with antimicrobial treatments. Many playground turf products include antimicrobial agents in the infill material itself, providing ongoing protection against bacteria and mold growth. This is especially important in Georgia’s warm, humid climate where organic surfaces can develop biological issues quickly.

Year-Round Usability

Georgia’s weather creates a frustrating cycle for daycare operators. After rain, natural grass and dirt areas may be unusable for hours or even days. During drought, dust creates respiratory concerns. In winter, dormant brown grass provides no cushioning and poor aesthetics. Artificial turf is usable within minutes after rain (proper drainage is key), generates no dust, stays green year-round, and maintains consistent cushioning in every season.

This matters because outdoor play time isn’t optional for children—it’s essential for development, and licensing requirements often mandate specific amounts of daily outdoor time. Having a reliable outdoor surface that’s always ready for play helps facilities meet these requirements regardless of weather conditions.

Cost Analysis for Facilities

A 3,000 sq ft playground turf installation with appropriate shock-absorbing padding runs $30,000-$50,000 depending on the fall height requirements and base conditions. That’s a significant investment, but compare it to the ongoing costs of maintaining other surfaces: annual re-sodding ($3,000-$8,000), weekly professional maintenance ($5,000-$10,000/year), wood mulch replacement every 1-2 years ($3,000-$6,000 per cycle), and the liability exposure from inadequate fall protection surfaces.

Most daycare and school installations pay for themselves within 3-5 years through eliminated maintenance costs. The 15-20 year lifespan of quality playground turf means 10-15 years of net savings after the break-even point.

What Parents Notice

When parents tour a daycare facility, the outdoor play area makes a strong impression. A muddy, patchy yard suggests neglect—even if the indoor program is excellent. A clean, green, well-maintained turf surface communicates investment in the children’s experience. Several of our daycare clients have told us that their turf installation directly contributed to increased enrollment. Parents see it and immediately understand that this facility takes the outdoor experience seriously.
