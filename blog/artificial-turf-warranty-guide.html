Artificial Turf Warranty Guide: What's Covered and What's Not

By Dusty Broadhead | March 1, 2026

Warranties on artificial turf can be confusing. There are manufacturer warranties, installer warranties, and a lot of fine print. After 20+ years in this business, I’ve processed plenty of warranty claims—some successful, some not—and I want to help you understand what protection you’re actually getting before you buy.

Two Separate Warranties

Most professional turf installations come with two distinct warranties that cover different things.

Manufacturer Warranty

This covers defects in the turf product itself. Typical coverage includes UV degradation beyond normal expectations (excessive fading or fiber brittleness), backing delamination (the fibers separating from the backing material), fiber defects (abnormal shedding, splitting, or disintegration), and color consistency issues (one section significantly different from another in the same roll).

Manufacturer warranties typically run 8-15 years for residential products, with premium products sometimes carrying 15-20 year warranties. Most are prorated after the initial period, meaning coverage decreases as the turf ages.

Installer Warranty

This covers the quality of the installation work. Typical coverage includes seam failures (seams coming apart), base settling (uneven surfaces developing due to improper compaction), drainage issues resulting from installation errors, and edge separation (turf pulling away from borders).

Installer warranties vary widely—from 1 year with fly-by-night operators to 5-10 years with established companies. At LawnLogic Turf, we stand behind our installation work because we’ve been here for over two decades and plan to be here for decades more.

What Warranties Typically Don’t Cover

This is where people get surprised. Common exclusions include:

  • Normal wear and tear: Gradual fiber flattening and fading from regular use is expected and not a defect. A 10-year-old turf that looks 10 years old isn’t a warranty claim.
  • Pet damage: Most manufacturer warranties exclude damage from animal urine, feces, or digging. Pet-specific turf may have modified coverage, but it’s rarely comprehensive.
  • Heat damage: Melting from reflected sunlight (off windows), grills, fire pits, or other heat sources is excluded. This is considered user error, not a product defect.
  • Improper maintenance: If you never brush, never rinse, and let organic debris accumulate, the resulting degradation isn’t covered.
  • Acts of nature: Flood damage, fallen trees, lightning strikes, and severe weather events are typically excluded.
  • Unauthorized modifications: If someone other than the original installer makes changes to the turf (cutting, reseaming, adding chemicals), the warranty may be voided.

Red Flags in Warranty Language

Watch out for these warning signs when reviewing turf warranties:

"Lifetime warranty": This almost never means what consumers think. Read the fine print—it usually means the "lifetime of the product" as defined by the manufacturer, which might be 10 years. Or it covers only catastrophic failure, not normal degradation.

Heavy proration: A 15-year warranty that’s prorated from year 3 means you’re paying most of the replacement cost yourself by year 8. Compare the actual coverage schedule, not just the warranty duration.

No installer warranty: If your installer doesn’t offer their own workmanship warranty separate from the manufacturer warranty, that’s a red flag. It means they don’t stand behind their work.

Claim requirements: Some warranties require professional maintenance at specified intervals to remain valid. If you don’t have documentation of that maintenance, the claim gets denied. Know these requirements upfront.

How to Protect Your Investment

  • Keep all documentation. Save your purchase receipt, warranty certificates, product specifications, and installation details. You’ll need these for any claim.
  • Document the installation. Take photos during and after installation. If a problem develops later, these photos prove the original condition.
  • Follow maintenance guidelines. Most warranties require "reasonable maintenance." Brush monthly, rinse periodically, and keep records.
  • Report problems early. Don’t wait until a small issue becomes a big one. Most warranties require timely reporting of defects.
  • Choose an established installer. A warranty is only as good as the company behind it. An installer who’s been in business 20+ years is more likely to be around to honor their warranty than one who opened last year.
