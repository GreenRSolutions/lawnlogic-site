Artificial Turf Drainage: How It Works in Georgia's Rain

By Dusty Broadhead | March 16, 2026

Georgia gets significant rainfall—especially during spring and summer when we can see 4-6 inches of rain in a single month. When I talk to homeowners considering artificial turf, one of the first questions is always about drainage: "Won't water just sit on top? Won't my yard become a swamp?" It's a legitimate concern, and after 20+ years installing turf across North Georgia, I can tell you definitively: proper artificial turf drainage is superior to natural grass drainage in many cases.

In this guide, I'll explain exactly how artificial turf handles water, what makes drainage work in Georgia's rainy climate, and how to know if your installation has adequate drainage.

Understanding Artificial Turf Drainage

Let me start with a common misconception: artificial turf is not waterproof. The turf fibers themselves are slightly porous, and more importantly, there's a drainage system built underneath. Water flows through the turf, through the backing, and down through the base layers where it either percolates into the ground or is routed to drainage systems.

The Multi-Layer Drainage System

A properly installed artificial turf system has several layers, each serving drainage purposes:

  • Turf fibers and backing: Water flows quickly through. The backing is intentionally designed with drainage holes.
  • Infill material: Sand and rubber particles allow water to pass through while providing cushioning and support.
  • Drainage layer: Often a permeable fabric or geotextile that prevents soil from mixing with infill while allowing water through.
  • Base layer: Compacted granite, recycled asphalt, or similar material that both supports the turf and allows water movement.
  • Sub-base grading: Slope and preparation to direct water away from the installation.

When designed correctly, this system drains faster than natural grass. A natural lawn relies entirely on soil permeability, which in Georgia's clay-based soils is often poor. Artificial turf, with its engineered layers, drains more predictably.

Drainage Performance Numbers

Artificial turf should drain at a rate of 20-30 inches per hour. That's significant—meaning even heavy rainfall drains away rapidly. In comparison, natural grass in Georgia clay soil often drains at 2-5 inches per hour or slower. After a typical Georgia thunderstorm (1-2 inches of rain), properly installed artificial turf drains within 30-60 minutes.

That said, these numbers assume proper installation. A turf system installed without adequate base preparation or slope will drain much more slowly.

Georgia-Specific Drainage Challenges

North Georgia's terrain and soil present unique drainage challenges that most installers outside our region don't fully understand.

Clay Soil and Water Retention

Georgia's native red clay is beautiful but terrible for drainage. It compacts easily, doesn't absorb water well, and creates conditions where water sits on or near the surface. This is why natural grass struggles here—roots can't penetrate the clay, and water doesn't percolate. When we install artificial turf, we often need to address the clay underneath.

How We Handle It

LawnLogic Turf typically removes or loosens the top 4-6 inches of clay, replaces it with a drain-friendly base (usually granite dust or recycled asphalt), and installs proper slope. This typically adds cost, but it's the difference between a turf system that drains perfectly and one that develops standing water problems.

Elevation and Grade

Many Georgia properties have flat or low-lying areas where water naturally collects. If your yard has a low spot, water will sit there unless we address it during installation. This is why site preparation is so critical—we need to identify these natural water collection points and either regrade them or install dedicated drainage solutions.

Drainage Solutions for Problem Areas

Some properties have more serious drainage challenges. Here's how we address them:

French Drain Systems

For areas with chronic water problems, we install perforated drainage lines that route water away from the turf area. These are laid under the base layer, and water flows through them to daylight or a storm drain. Cost is $500-2,000 depending on the system size, but it's worth it for problem properties.

Raised Bed Installation

For seriously waterlogged properties, we sometimes raise the entire turf area 6-12 inches above natural grade. This ensures water can't pool underneath. It's more visible but solves the problem permanently.

Sump Pump Systems

In rare cases where underground water flow is significant, we install sump collection systems. Water is pumped away from the turf area to suitable drainage locations.

Pet Turf Drainage Advantages

Pet-specific artificial turf has even more aggressive drainage design than standard turf. This is important for pet owners in Georgia's humid climate. Superior drainage prevents urine from pooling, which prevents odors and keeps the turf fresh-smelling year-round. When homeowners say their pet turf smells worse than expected, it's almost always a drainage problem, not an issue with the turf itself.

Long-Term Drainage Maintenance

Installation is only the beginning. Proper drainage requires ongoing attention.

Regular Inspection

After heavy rains, check your turf for standing water. If water pools in any area, contact us—it indicates a drainage problem that needs addressing.

Keep Drainage Clean

If you have drainage lines or perimeter drains, ensure they remain clear. Debris can clog systems. We typically recommend professional inspection every 2-3 years on systems with French drains.

Monitor Vegetation Growth

Tree roots seeking water can sometimes compromise subsurface drainage systems. Monitor for unusual settling or changes in how your turf drains over time.

Signs Your Drainage System Isn't Working

Contact us immediately if you notice:

  • Standing water that doesn't drain within 1-2 hours after rain
  • Soft or soggy feeling areas where you sink slightly into the turf
  • Unusual odors or algae growth (indicators of moisture issues)
  • Settling or depression forming in the turf

These are all fixable issues, but they require professional assessment.

The Bottom Line on Drainage

Artificial turf, when properly installed with attention to drainage, is superior to natural grass for Georgia properties. The engineered system handles our rainfall better than nature can. The key word is "properly installed"—cheap installations that skip proper base preparation or ignore drainage will fail. Professional installation that addresses Georgia's specific soil and climate challenges delivers a system that handles whatever our weather throws at it.
