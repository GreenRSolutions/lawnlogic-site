Georgia Turf Installation Permits: What You Need to Know

By Dusty Broadhead | February 11, 2026

One of the most common questions we get from Georgia homeowners is whether they need a permit to install artificial turf. The short answer for most residential installations is no—but there are situations where permits or approvals are required. Here's a straightforward breakdown of when you need to worry about permits and when you don't.

Standard Residential Installations

For a typical residential artificial turf installation—replacing your existing lawn with turf in the same location and at the same grade—no building permit is required in most Georgia jurisdictions. Turf installation is classified as a landscaping improvement, similar to planting a garden or installing mulch beds. Cobb County, Cherokee County, Fulton County, and the cities within them generally do not require permits for this type of work.

This applies to front yards, backyards, side yards, and other standard lawn areas. As long as you're not significantly altering the grade of the land, changing drainage patterns that affect neighboring properties, or building structures, standard turf installation is permit-free.

When Permits May Be Required

Several scenarios related to turf installation can trigger permit requirements. If your project includes retaining walls over 4 feet in height, most Georgia counties require a building permit and engineered plans. If grading changes will alter drainage patterns affecting neighboring properties, a grading permit may be required. If your installation includes electrical work for lighting or irrigation modifications, electrical permits are needed. If the project involves work within a stream buffer, floodplain, or wetland area, environmental permits are required. And commercial installations may have different requirements depending on the jurisdiction and scope.

The key distinction is that the turf itself doesn't require a permit—it's the site work around the turf that sometimes does. If your installation involves only excavation, base preparation, and turf placement without significant grade changes or structural work, you're in the clear.

Utility Locates: Always Required

While not technically a "permit," calling Georgia 811 (or dialing 811) before any excavation is legally required in Georgia. This free service sends utility companies to mark the location of underground gas lines, water lines, electrical conduits, and telecommunications cables on your property. Excavation without utility locates can result in dangerous line strikes and significant liability.

We call 811 on every project, typically 3-5 business days before excavation begins. The markings help us plan our dig and avoid any underground infrastructure. This step is non-negotiable—it's both a legal requirement and a critical safety practice.

HOA Approvals vs. Government Permits

It's important to distinguish between government permits (issued by your county or city) and HOA approvals (required by your homeowners association). They're completely separate processes. You might not need a government permit but still need HOA approval, or vice versa. Many Georgia homeowners in HOA communities need approval from their architectural review committee before making any exterior changes, including turf installation.

We handle HOA submissions regularly and can help prepare your approval package with product samples, specifications, and installation plans. Getting HOA approval before starting work is essential—installing without approval in a community that requires it can result in forced removal at your expense.

County-Specific Notes

In Cobb County, standard residential turf installation doesn't require a permit. However, if your project includes a retaining wall or significant grading, contact Cobb County Community Development at (770) 528-2000 to confirm requirements. Cherokee County follows similar guidelines—landscaping improvements don't require permits, but structural additions or grading changes may. The City of Kennesaw, City of Marietta, City of Acworth, and City of Woodstock generally align with their county requirements for landscaping work.

For commercial properties, requirements vary more widely. Commercial turf installations may require site plan approval, stormwater management review, or other permitting depending on the scope and location. We coordinate with local building departments on commercial projects to ensure all requirements are met.

Setback and Easement Considerations

Even without a permit requirement, you need to respect property setbacks and utility easements. Installing turf within a utility easement is generally fine (since it can be removed if utility access is needed), but installing permanent edging, walls, or structures within easements can create problems. Review your property survey for easement locations before finalizing your turf layout.

Similarly, some jurisdictions have sight-line requirements at intersections—corner lots may have restrictions on the height of landscaping (including any berms or elevated turf areas) within a certain distance of the corner to maintain driver visibility.
